Vago Jozsef

József Vágó ( Oradea , 1877 , December 23. - Sali-de-Béarn , 1947 , June 7 ) was a Hungarian architect , a prominent figure, together with his brother László, of Art Nouveau architecture . His son, Pierre Vago, is also an architect.


Biography


He was born in a family with nine children from Oradea . The parents were assimilated Jewish citizens and eventually moved to Budapest. As a child, József wanted to become an architect, a passion he passed on to his brother, László.



In 1902, after his boss started a fierce professional dispute with Ödön Lechner, who was especially respected by József Vágó, the latter left the company where he worked and opened a joint office with his brother, László. That was until 1911, and since all the plans made up to that point had been jointly signed, it is not clear what the role of each was. Most of their joint works are residential buildings in Budapest: the first in Síp Street no. 16, from Erzsébetváros (rom: Elisabeta neighborhood), 1902. While their major projects (e.g., the Vértanú Memorial Museum in Arad, 1901-1902, Szabadka City Hall, 1905, Rudas and Sáros Public Baths in Budapest, 1905) explicitly show Lechner's influence, the houses they built are moving away. more and more of the Hungarian Secession style, in the geometrizing direction represented by Otto Wagner. This is illustrated by the streets of Visegrad (Hungary, 1903) and the houses in Boráros Square on Mester Street, built in 1905-1906, and the Gutenberg House, which was also completed at that time. The two architects quickly became famous throughout Europe
